Chapter Three

IgE vs IgG: The Two Antibodies

One causes allergies. The other blocks them.

Your immune system produces different types of antibodies. Each has a different job. Two of them — IgE and IgG — tell opposite stories when it comes to allergies.

IgE

Immunoglobulin E

The allergy antibody. When IgE binds to an allergen, it triggers mast cells to release histamine — causing the sneezing, swelling, and anaphylaxis we associate with allergic reactions.

IgG

Immunoglobulin G

The workhorse antibody. IgG handles most infections. When it binds to an allergen, nothing dramatic happens — it simply neutralises the substance without triggering inflammation.

Both antibodies can recognise the same target. The difference is what happens after recognition.

The Mast Cell Problem

IgE doesn't just float around in your blood. It sits on the surface of mast cells — specialised immune cells packed with histamine and other inflammatory chemicals. When an allergen binds to IgE on a mast cell, the cell "degranulates" — releasing its contents explosively.

IgE-mediated allergy: when an allergen binds to IgE antibodies sitting on a mast cell, the cell releases histamine, causing allergic symptoms.

This is why allergies are so quick and dramatic. The mast cells are already armed with IgE, just waiting for the allergen to arrive. The moment it does, they explode.

IgG: The Interceptor

IgG works differently. It floats freely in the blood and can bind to allergens before they reach the IgE-armed mast cells. When IgG grabs an allergen, it neutralises it harmlessly — no mast cell activation, no histamine, no allergic reaction.

IgG-mediated protection: free-floating IgG antibodies can grab allergens in the bloodstream before they reach mast cells, preventing allergic reactions entirely.

An Analogy

Think of IgE as a landmine — devastating when triggered, but it sits and waits. IgG is more like a roving security patrol — it intercepts threats before they can cause damage. If you have enough IgG patrolling for a particular allergen, the landmines never get set off.

The Pet Shop Mice's Secret

Now we can understand what was happening in the pet shop mice. They had high levels of IgG antibodies that could bind to common allergens. When exposed to those allergens, the IgG intercepted them before they could trigger an IgE response.

Laboratory mice lacked this IgG library. When they encountered an allergen for the first time, the only response available was the IgE pathway — leading directly to allergy.

The mechanism: Pet shop mice are protected not because they lack an immune response, but because they have the right immune response — a library of IgG antibodies that neutralise allergens before they can trigger IgE and mast cell activation.

But here's the puzzle: the pet shop mice had never been exposed to the specific allergens used in the experiment. So how did they have IgG antibodies ready to block them?

The answer involves something called cross-reactivity — and it's the key to the whole story.
