Chapter One

The Allergy Epidemic

Why allergies have exploded — and why genetics can't explain it

Something strange has happened over the past century. Allergies — to food, to pollen, to dust — haven't just increased. They've exploded.

These numbers demand an explanation. And the obvious candidate — genetics — doesn't work.

The Genetic Problem

Human genes don't change that quickly. Evolution operates over thousands of years, not decades. If allergies have tripled since your grandparents were young, it's not because our DNA has mutated. Something in our environment has changed.

Three Observations That Point to Environment

  1. Geography matters. Allergy rates vary wildly by location — even among genetically similar populations. Move from a low-allergy country to a high-allergy one, and your risk goes up.
  2. Migration changes risk. People who emigrate from regions with low allergy rates start developing allergies at the rates of their new home — especially if they move young.
  3. The speed of change. The rise has been too rapid to be explained by genetics. Whatever is causing this operates on a timescale of years, not generations.

The Hygiene Hypothesis

There's a theory that's been around since the 1980s: the "hygiene hypothesis." The basic idea is that our immune systems evolved expecting a certain level of microbial exposure — infections, bacteria, parasites — and modern cleanliness has disrupted that expectation.

It's not that dirt is good or that infections are beneficial. It's subtler than that. The immune system needs to be calibrated. Without the right early exposures, it might miscalibrate — treating harmless things (pollen, peanuts) as threats.

The hygiene hypothesis in brief: our modern environment may deprive the immune system of the microbial exposure it evolved to expect.

The hypothesis is supported by epidemiological evidence. Children who grow up on farms have lower rates of allergies. So do children with older siblings, children who attend nursery early, and children who have pets. The common thread: more microbial exposure in early life.

But How?

The hygiene hypothesis describes a correlation, not a mechanism. How exactly does early microbial exposure protect against allergies? What happens in the immune system? And why does it matter so much when that exposure happens?

These questions remained unanswered for decades. Until a group of researchers decided to study mice from the pet shop.

The puzzle: Allergies have exploded too fast to be genetic. Environmental changes are driving it — but the mechanism was unclear. The research in this paper uses a clever model system to uncover how early microbial exposure shapes the immune response.
