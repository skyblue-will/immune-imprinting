Chapter Six

Can Allergies Be Undone?

The conventional view — and why it might be wrong

There's a common belief in immunology: once you're sensitised to an allergen, you're allergic for life. The IgE antibodies persist. The mast cells remain primed. At best, you can manage symptoms; you can't truly reverse the underlying condition.

This research challenges that assumption.

The Transfer Experiment

The researchers designed a particularly clever experiment. They took already-allergic laboratory mice — animals that had been sensitised and had demonstrated allergic responses — and asked: could exposure to pet shop mice's microbiome reverse their allergies?

The answer was nuanced but hopeful.

Even mice with established allergies showed improvement after exposure to the pet shop microbiome — suggesting allergies may not be as permanent as previously thought.

What Changed?

The already-allergic mice showed several changes after microbial exposure:

IgG Increased

The mice developed new IgG antibodies that could intercept allergens, even though they hadn't had the benefit of early exposure.

Symptoms Reduced

Allergic reactions were less severe, though not completely eliminated. The mast cells were still there, but they weren't being triggered as easily.

The protection wasn't as complete as in mice exposed early in life. The IgE was still present. But the balance had shifted — more IgG intercepting allergens before they could reach the IgE-armed mast cells.

Why Isn't It Complete?

Several factors made adult reversal harder than early prevention:

  • Existing IgE persists: The allergy antibodies don't disappear. They continue sitting on mast cells, ready to trigger. New IgG has to compete with established IgE.
  • Memory cells: The immune system has already "learned" to make IgE against the allergen. This memory makes it easier to ramp up IgE production again.
  • Closed window: The immune system is less plastic in adulthood. It takes more exposure to shift the balance, and the shifts are smaller.

An Important Caveat

This was demonstrated in mice, under controlled laboratory conditions. The researchers used specific protocols for microbial transfer. This doesn't mean that humans with allergies should seek out "dirty" environments — that could be dangerous. But it does suggest that the immune system retains more flexibility than previously assumed.

The Hope and the Limit

The finding challenges the "once allergic, always allergic" dogma. The immune system can be rebalanced, at least partially, even after allergies have developed. The IgG pathway can be activated in adulthood.

But the finding also underscores why prevention is better than treatment. Early exposure creates a baseline that's hard to replicate later. The critical window exists for a reason — the developing immune system is uniquely receptive during that period.

The takeaway: Allergies may not be as permanent as once thought. Adult microbial exposure can partially rebalance the immune system. But early exposure remains far more effective — prevention is easier than reversal.

This has real implications for how we think about treating allergies in humans. The final chapter explores what this research might mean for us.
